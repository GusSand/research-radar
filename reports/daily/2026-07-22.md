## Top 10 (priority order)

### 1. [**CircuitKIT: Circuit Discovery, Evaluation, and Application Toolkit for Mechanistic Interpretability**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19317)
- **Authors / venue:** Pratinav Seth, Hem Gosalia, Aditya Kasliwal, Vinay Kumar Sankarapu — arXiv preprint (July 21, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Submitted yesterday; addresses the most pressing infrastructure gap in mech-interp practice — the absence of a unified end-to-end pipeline spanning discovery, evaluation, and downstream application of circuits.
- **Technical summary:** CircuitKIT provides a typed, serializable circuit representation that connects every stage of the circuit-analysis workflow: a suite of discovery algorithms (EAP, ACDC, and others), declarative interfaces for mapping arbitrary structured datasets into discovery tasks without manual prompt-writing, complementary diagnostics for measuring circuit faithfulness and completeness, and downstream application modules for pruning, targeted editing, activation steering, and selective fine-tuning. Because circuits are serialized, results from different discovery methods are directly comparable and composable. The library is source-available and is designed so that adding a new discovery algorithm requires implementing a single typed interface.

### 2. [**Agent Data Injection Attacks are Realistic Threats to AI Agents**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05120)
- **Authors / venue:** Woohyuk Choi, Juhee Kim, Taehyun Kang, Jihyeon Jeong, Luyi Xing, Byoungyoung Lee (Seoul National University; Largosoft; UIUC) — arXiv preprint (July 6, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Defines a new attack class — agent data injection (ADI) — distinct from instruction injection; demonstrates concrete exploitation of Claude Code, Codex, and Gemini CLI in production; easily bypasses current indirect prompt-injection defenses.
- **Technical summary:** ADI injects malicious data disguised as trusted agent-context material: security-critical metadata (resource identifiers, data-origin fields) or tool-call/response format tokens. Because the agent treats this data as structurally authoritative rather than as untrusted instructions, existing defenses that sanitize instruction-style content do not fire. The authors demonstrate two attack classes: arbitrary click attacks on web agents (Claude in Chrome, Antigravity, Nanobrowser) and remote code execution / supply-chain attacks on coding agents (Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI). Evaluation across off-the-shelf LLMs shows ADI is effective in both standalone-LLM and multi-turn agentic settings, making it an immediate practical threat to deployed AI systems.

### 3. [**Robust Harmful Features Under Jailbreak Attacks: Mechanistic Evidence from Attention Head Specialization in Large Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28153)
- **Authors / venue:** Yanchen Yin, Dongqi Han, Linghui Li — arXiv preprint (June 26, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Provides the strongest mechanistic account to date of *how* jailbreaks bypass refusal — not by eliminating safety signals, but by selectively suppressing specific early-layer attention heads — and converts this finding into a training-free detector.
- **Technical summary:** The paper identifies two functionally distinct attention head classes: Adversarially Compromised Heads (ACHs), concentrated in early layers and suppressed when attacks succeed, and Safety-Aligned Heads (SAHs), located in mid-layers, whose activations remain robust even during successful jailbreaks. Token-level attribution reveals that ACH suppression is driven specifically by attack-template tokens (not by harmful content), mechanistically explaining why attack formatting alone is sufficient to bypass refusal. Ablation studies confirm causal necessity: suppressing a small number of ACHs induces jailbreak-like behavior on normally refused inputs, while ablating SAHs degrades mid-layer safety signals. Reading SAH activations without any fine-tuning yields competitive jailbreak detection performance with strong adversarial robustness.

### 4. [**AgentLens: Interpretable Safety Steering via Mechanistic Subspaces for Multi-Turn Coding Agent**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22673)
- **Authors / venue:** (multi-institution) — arXiv preprint (June 27, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Bridges mech-interp and agent safety with a practical runtime defense; introduces the first benchmark specifically designed for mechanistic safety evaluation of multi-turn coding agents.
- **Technical summary:** AgentLens proposes Mechanistic Subspace Intervention and Steering (MSIS), a white-box defense that identifies harmful execution states at each agent step by probing step-level hidden representations and mitigates unsafe behavior by intervening in a 10-dimensional subspace within a single transformer layer — a deliberately minimal footprint to preserve task utility. Unsafe intent is detected before tool execution, not after. The authors introduce the Mechanistic Agent Safety (MAS) benchmark, comprising 194 fully annotated multi-turn execution trajectories across diverse coding tasks using LLaMA-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, and Gemma-2-9B as backends. MAS reveals that existing single-turn or jailbreak-focused interpretability methods fail to capture the evolving risk dynamics of multi-turn agent execution.

### 5. [**Steering Without Breaking: Mechanistically Informed Interventions for Discrete Diffusion Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10971)
- **Authors / venue:** Hanhan Zhou, Shamik Roy, Rashmi Gangadharaiah (AWS AI Labs) — arXiv preprint (May 8, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** First SAE-based mechanistic study of *when* different attributes crystallize during dLLM denoising; yields an adaptive steering schedule that substantially outperforms uniform-step baselines while preserving output quality.
- **Technical summary:** The paper trains TopK sparse autoencoders on residual-stream activations at each denoising step across four DLMs spanning 124M–8B parameters, finding that distinct attributes commit on sharply different schedules: topic solidifies within the first 2% of denoising steps, whereas sentiment drifts gradually over ~20% of the process. Uniform-step steering wastes intervention budget on timesteps where the target attribute is already fixed or has yet to emerge, causing quality degradation that compounds under multi-attribute control. The authors derive a closed-form efficiency criterion that predicts which models benefit most from adaptive scheduling, and an adaptive framework that concentrates interventions at the mechanistically identified commitment windows. Adaptive steering achieves strong single- and multi-attribute control with lower quality cost and less cross-attribute interference than four baselines across all four DLMs.

### 6. [**Many Circuits, One Mechanism: Input Variation and Evaluation Granularity in Circuit Discovery**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06267)
- **Authors / venue:** Alireza Bayat Makou, Jingcheng Niu, Subhabrata Dutta, Iryna Gurevych — arXiv preprint (June 4, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Challenges a widely held assumption in circuit-discovery methodology — that structural differences between circuits imply functional differences — with controlled experiments across 75 circuits; has direct implications for how mech-interp results should be interpreted and reported.
- **Technical summary:** The authors hold the task fixed (Literal Sequence Copying) while varying input statistics across four token-frequency bands and a control condition, running edge-attribution patching on five Pythia models from 70M to 1.4B. They discover "phantom specialization": circuits discovered under different input distributions look structurally distinct but implement the same underlying computation. Evidence includes: (1) band-specific edges transfer broadly across other frequency bands, (2) a core subgraph shared across most bands recovers at least 99% of the full circuit's performance, and (3) causal interchange interventions confirm that internal representations are interchangeable across bands. The finding is an artifact of evaluation granularity: source-level evaluation inflates apparent faithfulness and masks this many-to-one mapping from structure to function; edge-level evaluation reveals it.

### 7. [**Subspace-Aware Sparse Autoencoders for Effective Mechanistic Interpretability**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06333)
- **Authors / venue:** arXiv preprint (June 4, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Proposes a principled architectural fix for a well-known SAE failure mode (feature splitting / absorption) by replacing single-vector decoders with learned subspaces, demonstrating improvements on GPT-2 Small and Mistral-7B.
- **Technical summary:** Standard SAEs assign each latent a single decoder direction, implicitly assuming features are one-dimensional — a mismatch with the multi-dimensional structure of model representations that provably induces feature splitting (one concept spread across multiple latents) and absorption (one latent picking up unrelated concepts). Subspace-Aware Sparse Autoencoders (SASA) replace each decoder direction with a learned low-rank subspace and enforce block sparsity via Top-s group gating; a nuclear-norm regularizer adapts the effective rank of each subspace during training. Evaluated on residual-stream activations of GPT-2 Small and Mistral-7B, SASA substantially reduces absorption as measured by the first-letter benchmark, a standard diagnostic for absorption failure. SASA is also sample-efficient: it achieves this at roughly half the token budget of standard externally trained SAEs.

### 8. [**The Balkanization of Execution-Security Research for AI Coding Agents: Isolation, Access Control, and Time-of-Check-to-Time-of-Use Vulnerabilities**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05743)
- **Authors / venue:** Mohammadreza Rashidi — arXiv preprint (July 5, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Systematizes 39 execution-security papers across 17 categories, confirms 4 patched CVEs in production agent harnesses, and identifies 5 structural gaps across the fragmented research landscape.
- **Technical summary:** The paper performs a systematic literature review of sandbox isolation, capability and access control, policy enforcement, TOCTOU races, MCP threats, identity delegation, execution provenance, network egress control, and static analysis of agent-generated code from 2023–2026. By directly verifying each paper against its source (rather than relying on secondary summaries), it surfaces five crosscutting gaps: isolation architectures and capability models are evaluated against attacker capability but almost never against each other; policy-enforcement studies report failure rates of 69%–98%; TOCTOU and MCP threats are analyzed as separate literatures despite being instances of the same state-validation problem; all enforcement mechanisms assume an honest policy author; and benign out-of-scope agent actions remain a measured but largely unaddressed failure mode. Four disclosed and patched CVEs directly affecting production agent harnesses are documented.

### 9. [**A Geometric View for Understanding Concept Learning and Neuron Interpretation in Sparse Autoencoders**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07007)
- **Authors / venue:** Chenhao Zhang, Chris Lin, Su-In Lee — arXiv preprint (June 5, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Provides the first formal unified framework for SAE concept learning and neuron interpretation, with geometric conditions, error bounds, and capacity constraints that explain empirically observed failure modes (feature splitting, absorption, families) from first principles.
- **Technical summary:** The paper formalizes concepts as sets of data points and casts concept learning as a set-alignment problem between human-defined and model-induced concepts. Three increasingly strong learning notions are defined — detection, separation, and approximation — each with geometric conditions determining when a concept can be captured by individual neurons versus multi-neuron units. The framework yields formal capacity constraints on SAE size and sparsity, and provides set-theoretic derivations for feature splitting (one concept needing multiple latents), feature absorption (one latent capturing unrelated concepts), feature families (hierarchical groupings), and hierarchical concepts. Connections between concept learning and neuron interpretation are formalized through formal concept analysis, showing the two directions need not agree and that their many-to-many structure can be organized by concept lattices. Experiments with ReLU and Top-K SAEs on synthetic data illustrate theory predictions.

### 10. [**Adaptive Evaluation of Out-of-Band Defenses Against Prompt Injection in LLM Agents**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26479)
- **Authors / venue:** Praneeth Narisetty, Shiva Nagendra Babu Kore, Uday Kumar Reddy Kattamanchi, Jayaram Kumarapu (LaunchSafe Research) — arXiv preprint (June 25, 2026)
- **Why it ranks here:** Provides structured comparison of second-generation out-of-band defenses (CaMeL, FIDES, Progent, RTBAS, FORGE) through classical security frameworks; points out an evaluation methodology concern echoing historical XSS/SQLi defense trajectories.
- **Technical summary:** Out-of-band defenses enforce security outside the LLM — via capability systems, information-flow labels, and reference monitors — and several claim near-elimination of prompt injection on AgentDojo. This paper organizes these systems as instances of Biba integrity protection, reference monitoring, and least privilege, yielding a structured comparison of coverage. The authors then evaluate defenses adaptively on real-world agent scenarios (Slack, banking, workspace) and find that Progent suppressed all standard-benchmark attacks, but the adaptive attacker with knowledge of the defense did not achieve consistent bypass either — suggesting the defenses are currently robust to known attack patterns but that the second-generation systems have been evaluated against the same fixed benchmark as the first. The paper calls for adversarially co-evolved evaluation rather than static-benchmark certification.
